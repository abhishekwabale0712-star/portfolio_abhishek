NoBroker Consumer Service Pod

A service-design and product-operations case study focused on creating clearer ownership, faster issue resolution, and measurable improvements in the consumer support journey.

Context

NoBroker operates in a high-touch consumer journey where users need help across discovery, booking, payment, movement, documentation, issue resolution, and post-service support.

The opportunity was to frame a pod-based service model that gives one focused team ownership over a defined part of the consumer journey instead of allowing tickets to move through fragmented queues.

Case formatNotion-backed product case study with service-design and operations thinking.
Primary goalImprove consumer resolution speed, visibility, accountability, and service quality.

The problem in detail

Consumer-service journeys often break when ownership is unclear. A customer may raise an issue, repeat the same context across multiple touchpoints, wait without clear status, and experience delays because internal teams do not share one operating view.

The core product problem is not only ticket volume. It is the lack of a clear service operating model: who owns the case, what the next action is, when it should be escalated, and how the team learns from repeated failure patterns.

User problem

Customers do not want to chase support teams or explain their issue repeatedly.

Team problem

Support teams need issue ownership, prioritization, escalation clarity, and service-health visibility.

Business problem

Slow or unclear resolution reduces trust, increases repeat contacts, and creates avoidable operational cost.

Product problem

The service system needs to convert scattered support work into a measurable pod workflow.

My role

  • Mapped the consumer-service journey and identified handoff-heavy moments.
  • Defined a pod ownership model for a focused set of consumer problems.
  • Outlined prioritization and escalation logic for issue queues.
  • Connected service quality to measurable operational metrics.
  • Structured the case as a product operating model, not just a support process.

Approach

I framed the solution around a consumer-service pod: a cross-functional unit responsible for a specific service journey, with clear ownership from issue intake to closure.

01

Map the consumer journey

Identify where consumers raise issues, where they lose visibility, and where internal handoffs create delays.

02

Define pod ownership

Clarify which issue categories the pod owns, what the SLA should be, and when a case needs escalation.

03

Design the pod workflow

Create a queue-based workflow for intake, triage, owner assignment, resolution, closure, and learning loops.

04

Measure service health

Track speed, quality, reopen rate, repeat-contact rate, backlog aging, escalation health, and CSAT.

A consumer-service pod works when ownership, visibility, and measurable outcomes sit inside the same operating rhythm.

Proposed solution

The solution is a pod control center that gives the team one view of inflow, owner, priority, SLA risk, resolution status, and recurring issue themes.

Issue intake

Classify incoming cases by journey stage, urgency, and issue type.

Pod ownership

Assign every case to an owner and make the next action visible.

Escalation logic

Trigger escalation when SLA risk, customer sentiment, or operational blocker crosses a threshold.

Learning loop

Use repeated issue themes to improve the product journey, help content, and operations playbooks.

Product glimpse

A pod dashboard for service ownership and resolution health.

Pod control center Inflow, ownership, escalations, and resolution quality visible in one operating view.

Impact

  • Improves resolution speed by reducing unclear ownership and repeated handoffs.
  • Improves customer trust by making case status and next action clearer.
  • Reduces repeat contacts because customers do not need to restate the same issue.
  • Creates measurable service-health visibility for support and product leaders.
  • Turns recurring support themes into product and operations improvement opportunities.

What I learned

  • Service design needs both customer empathy and internal operating clarity.
  • Most support problems are not just communication problems; they are ownership-system problems.
  • A pod model only works if the team has the authority and metrics to solve problems end to end.
  • Good service dashboards should show what action is needed next, not just report ticket counts.
