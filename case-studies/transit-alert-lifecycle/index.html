Case Study 02

Helping transit agencies keep service alerts accurate

A product concept for an Alert Lifecycle Manager that helps agency staff identify service alerts requiring review, update, extension, or closure before riders see outdated information.

Product Management
Transit Operations
RICE: 16.2

Context

Transit agencies operate public transportation systems and manage routes, schedules, stops, fares, service disruptions, and rider-facing communication. When delays, detours, maintenance work, weather events, or stop closures happen, riders depend on timely and accurate alerts to plan their journeys.

The platform explored in this case acts as a central system where transit agencies can create and manage service alerts and transit data, then publish that information across rider-facing channels.

For ridersTimely alerts reduce uncertainty and help people make better travel decisions.
For agenciesA centralized workflow reduces the complexity of managing alerts and data.

The problem in detail

Transit agencies are experiencing increasing operational complexity in managing service alerts while trying to provide riders with timely and accurate transit information.

The alert lifecycle - creation, publishing, monitoring, updates, extensions, and resolution - depends heavily on manual effort. As disruption volume increases, active alerts become harder to monitor. Staff may forget to close alerts after resolution, may miss alerts nearing expiration, or may spend time manually checking which alerts require attention.

This creates slower communication, inconsistent information, and reduced confidence among riders. The deeper problem is not alert creation alone; it is maintaining alert accuracy after the alert has been published.

Pain point

Keeping alerts accurate requires ongoing manual monitoring.

Pain point

Identifying which active alerts need review becomes difficult as alert volume grows.

Pain point

Resolved disruptions may remain visible if staff forget to close the alert.

Rider impact

Outdated rider information reduces trust in transit communication.

Users and stakeholders

01

Maya - agency communications officer

Creates and updates alerts during disruptions. She is non-technical, works under time pressure, and is accountable for accuracy during multiple simultaneous events.

02

Alex - daily rider

Checks transit information before leaving and expects it to be current. If the website shows nothing unusual, he assumes service is normal.

03

John - account manager

Onboards agencies, trains their staff, handles support tickets, and needs agencies to operate independently without constant hand-holding.

The goal is not to create more alerts. The goal is to keep active alerts accurate throughout their lifecycle.

Key assumptions

Since I did not have access to internal analytics or live user interviews, I based this case on product exploration and publicly available transit-agency resources.

  • Transit agencies frequently experience disruptions requiring timely rider communication.
  • Agency staff are responsible for creating, updating, and resolving service alerts throughout the disruption lifecycle.
  • Riders rely on service alerts to make travel decisions and expect information to be current.
  • Active alerts can remain published after resolution if staff forget to update or deactivate them.
  • Maintaining timely, accurate, and consistent communication is a common operational challenge.

Why this feature won

I compared three feature ideas using RICE: Alert Lifecycle Manager, AI-Powered Alert Assistant, and Alert Insights Dashboard. The Alert Lifecycle Manager ranked first because it solved the core lifecycle maintenance problem directly.

Feature: Alert Lifecycle Manager

The feature helps transit agency staff identify alerts that require attention after they have been published. It provides a review queue for alerts requiring action, highlights alerts that may need updates or closure, and enables staff to keep rider information accurate and up to date.

Impact

  • Improves information accuracy by helping agencies identify alerts requiring review, updates, or closure.
  • Improves operational efficiency because staff no longer need to manually monitor every active alert.
  • Improves scalability as agencies can manage more alerts without a proportional increase in manual effort.
  • Improves rider experience because riders receive more accurate and current transit information.
  • Improves product value by expanding the system from alert creation to alert quality management.

Success metrics

North Star MetricPercentage of active alerts that remain accurate and up to date throughout their lifecycle.
Initiative MetricPercentage of alerts reviewed, updated, or closed before becoming outdated.
  • Reduce the percentage of outdated active alerts by 50%.
  • Increase alerts reviewed before expiration from 40% to 80%.
  • Reduce alerts remaining active after disruption resolution.
  • Reduce average time spent monitoring alerts.
  • Reduce rider complaints related to inaccurate service alerts.

Rollout strategy and roadmap

01

Launch with 2-3 existing transit agencies, monitor alert review rates and adoption, and collect qualitative feedback from agency staff.

02

General availability

Enable the feature for all agencies with onboarding guides, product walkthroughs, and success-metric tracking.

03

Optimization

Add smart recommendations, deeper alert analytics, collaboration workflows, and expanded reporting.

What I learned

This case reinforced that the highest-impact product opportunity is not always the most visible feature. In this problem, alert creation was already possible. The bigger value was maintaining information quality after publishing.

I also learned to separate activity metrics from outcome metrics. Counting alerts created would measure work done, but alert accuracy across the lifecycle measures whether riders can actually trust the information.
